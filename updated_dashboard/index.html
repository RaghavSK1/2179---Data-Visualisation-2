One of Earth's Most Diverse Ecosystems

Over 30,000 known species — and counting

The Great Barrier Reef is one of the most species-rich ecosystems on Earth. More than 12,000 invertebrate species, 1,625 bony fishes, 411 hard coral species, and 15 seagrasses — including one found nowhere else on Earth — make their home here.

Yet the 2024 Outlook Report reveals a troubling pattern: eight of the sixteen assessed species groups are in poor condition, including sharks and rays, marine turtles, seabirds, dugongs, and the invertebrates that form the base of the reef food web.

Only mangroves remain in very good condition. Hard corals show tentative signs of improvement after years of bleaching — but remain extremely vulnerable.

🐠 1,625 fish species — more than any other reef system in the southern hemisphere
🌿 15 seagrass species, including Halophila tricostata — endemic to the GBR
02 — Three Decades of Change

The Long Decline — And a Fragile Recovery

Stressors have compounded since the late 1990s

Since 1993, the GBR has experienced six mass bleaching events, three major Crown-of-Thorns Starfish (COTS) outbreak waves, and multiple severe cyclones. Each disturbance leaves the reef with less time to recover before the next arrives.

The chart to the right shows the GBR-wide coral cover trajectory alongside cumulative COTS counts. The dual crash of 2010–2012 — driven simultaneously by Cyclone Yasi (2011) and a sustained COTS outbreak — reduced live coral cover to its lowest recorded level of just 13%.

"The window between disturbances is closing. Reefs that once had decades to recover now have years — or months."

— AIMS Long-Term Monitoring Programme (2023)

A patchy picture of health

The Great Barrier Reef is not one uniform ecosystem — it is an archipelago of over 3,000 individual reefs stretching 2,300 km along Queensland's coast. Survey data from AIMS's Long-Term Monitoring Programme reveals striking spatial variation in live coral cover: offshore northern reefs show some of the healthiest conditions, while inshore central and southern sections carry the scars of repeated disturbance.

The 2022–23 monitoring season recorded the highest reef-wide coral cover since systematic surveys began — a fragile sign of recovery after the catastrophic bleaching events of 2016 and 2017. But this recovery is uneven, and the ocean conditions that enabled it are already shifting.

04 — The Ocean is Changing

Warming Waters, Toxic Runoff

Two stressors that strike together

Coral bleaching is triggered when sea surface temperatures exceed the local maximum by even 1–2°C for several weeks. But warming alone does not tell the full story. Agricultural runoff from Queensland's catchments floods inshore reefs with excess nutrients (measured as Chlorophyll-a concentration), weakening corals' capacity to survive thermal stress.

The stressors do not act alone — they compound

The scatter reveals what the combination of heat and runoff does to coral cover. Offshore reefs face the highest thermal stress but little runoff — and hold the best coral cover. Inshore reefs carry the highest Chlorophyll-a loads from catchment runoff, suppressing coral even when temperatures are modest.

The darkest red points cluster in the compound-stress zone where both stressors are elevated simultaneously. Elevated Chlorophyll-a weakens corals' thermal tolerance by an estimated 1–2°C — a reef that might survive a moderate heatwave in clean water will bleach in nutrient-enriched conditions.

⚠ Reefs in the compound-stress zone average 16% live coral cover — less than half the 36% at clean offshore reefs
🌿 Halving catchment nutrient loads could raise coral thermal tolerance thresholds — buying reefs critical recovery time
05 — The Economic Reef

A $7 Billion Economy — and a Fragile One

Tourism dominates, but COVID exposed the vulnerability

Commercial marine tourism is the reef's largest dependent industry, contributing an estimated $5.7 billion to the Australian economy in 2015–16 and supporting 38,000 full-time equivalent reef-dependent jobs. By 2018–19 that figure was growing toward $7 billion — then COVID-19 border closures reduced it to roughly $3.3 billion in 2020, the most severe single-year shock the reef economy has ever experienced.

06 — Australians and Their Reef

More Than Scenery — A Living Resource

The reef supports millions of Australians

The GBR is not only an ecological system — it is the economic and cultural backbone of coastal Queensland. Around two million people live in the six NRM bioregions that border the reef, and recreational use is deeply embedded in local life: fishing, boating, diving, and island visits are among the most common outdoor activities in the region.

The SELTMP 2025 survey asked coastal Queenslanders how they use and value the reef. The results reveal significant variation by region — with Cape York residents most likely to fish offshore, while Whitsunday and Wet Tropics communities lean toward diving and tourism-adjacent activities.

07 — Perception, Values & Action

What Australians Think is Threatening the Reef

A gap between science and public perception

While scientists identify ocean warming and bleaching as the single greatest threat to the GBR, public surveys reveal a more complex picture. Respondents ranked Crown-of-Thorns Starfish, marine debris, and agricultural runoff as the top perceived threats — placing climate change further down the list.

Crucially, people who have visited the reef show a different threat profile: they are more aware of bleaching and heatwave risk, and rate agricultural runoff higher. This suggests that direct experience does shift environmental understanding.

Experience changes how we value the reef

People who have visited the reef consistently score its values higher across every category — from biodiversity to economic importance and iconic status. This "experience effect" is strongest for recreation and scientific value, where those who have visited rate the reef 0.4–0.6 points higher on a 10-point scale.

This matters for conservation: people who value the reef more are more likely to support protective policies. Ecotourism isn't just an economic activity — it builds the constituency for long-term reef protection.

"Those who visit the reef are more likely to see climate change as a serious threat to it — even if they don't rank it highest in overall severity."

— SELTMP 2025 Survey analysis

Experience converts into action — but slowly

The heatmap shows a striking pattern: the vast majority of people — regardless of how often they visit — report taking no protective action. This is consistent with the "value-action gap" identified in environmental psychology: people may care deeply about the reef without translating that care into behaviour.

However, those who visit the reef most frequently (weekly or more) show meaningfully higher rates of both independent action (e.g., reducing plastic use, donations) and formal participation (e.g., volunteering, submissions to reef management bodies). Proximity and experience matter.

This has real policy implications: investing in reef access and reef-literacy programs — particularly for regional communities — could be among the most cost-effective tools for building a conservation-active public.

Conclusion

The Reef's Future Depends on Both Nature and People

The Great Barrier Reef sits at a crossroads. Ecologically, it has shown a capacity to recover — but that capacity is being outpaced by the frequency and intensity of disturbances driven by climate change, land-based runoff, and biological threats. The 2022–23 record coral cover was a cause for cautious optimism; the 2024 mass bleaching was a reminder of how precarious that recovery remains.

Socially, the data offers a more hopeful signal. Australians who interact with the reef understand it better, value it more, and are more likely to act to protect it. This feedback loop — experience → values → action → protection — is the reef's most powerful social asset.

The challenge is to close the gap between ecological urgency and community action. The reef does not have time for incremental change. But with the right policies — reducing land runoff, accelerating emissions reductions, and investing in public reef literacy — there is still time to secure it for future generations.
