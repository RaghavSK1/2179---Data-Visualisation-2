The Great Barrier Reef

A Visual Narrative of Ecological Health and Human Connection

1. The Current State of the Reef

Live Coral Overview

Before examining the threats to the Great Barrier Reef (GBR), it is essential to understand its baseline health. This map visualises the mean live coral percentage recorded during the most recent Manta Tow surveys for each surveyed reef.

Areas heavily saturated in green indicate robust coral health, while red areas flag regions with severely depleted live coral cover, prompting a deeper dive into the ecological stressors responsible.

2. Ecological Stressors: Climate and Predators

Compound Stressors: Temperature and Chlorophyll

Coral health is heavily dictated by oceanic conditions. High Sea Surface Temperatures (SST) trigger widespread coral bleaching, while agricultural runoff (measured here by Chlorophyll-a concentration) degrades water quality and blocks vital sunlight. Use the interactive tools below to explore these dual stressors across different years and months.

Biological Threats: Crown-of-Thorns Starfish

Poor water quality (like high chlorophyll) often leads to outbreaks of the coral-eating Crown-of-Thorns Starfish (COTS). This map tracks the devastating biological impact of COTS over the years.

The size of the circles represents the mean COTS per tow, while the intense red colour scale reveals the resulting dead coral percentage. Use the slider to track historical outbreaks.

3. Human Connection: How We Use and Value the Reef

Recreational Use by Region

The Great Barrier Reef is not just an ecological wonder; it is a vital recreational hub for Australians. Click on the different Natural Resource Management (NRM) regions on the map below to discover what local residents are doing in their coastal waters (from fishing and boating to snorkelling).

Why Do We Value the Reef?

Public surveys reveal that our appreciation for the reef goes far beyond recreation. This dumbbell chart compares how people who have visited the reef vs. those who haven't score different reef values (such as aesthetic beauty, biodiversity, and economic importance).

Notice how direct experience with the reef shifts these average scores, highlighting the power of ecotourism in building environmental appreciation.

4. Public Perception and Protective Action

Perceived Threats to the Reef

Despite scientific consensus on climate change being the ultimate threat, public perception often varies. This bar chart ranks the severity of perceived threats out of 10. You can filter the data to see if actually visiting the reef changes a person's understanding of what endangers it most.

Does Experience Drive Action?

Finally, does living near or visiting the reef encourage protective behaviour? This heatmap explores the relationship between how frequently someone visits the reef and whether they have taken independent or formal action to protect it.

The concentration of respondents reveals whether physical proximity and experience translate into tangible environmental stewardship.
